The Museum of Moments: Returning to Reflection

After nearly five weeks away, sidelined by a little accident, I'm back to writing—full throttle, no brakes. That's just who I am.

In the quiet moments, my mind drifted to my exes. Among the three, one still lingers—not with sadness, but with a nostalgic, cinematic glow. With them, I collected memories like a squirrel hoards nuts for winter—the good, the bad, the embarrassingly messy—all carefully curated and filed away in a personal museum of romantic archaeology.

That chapter echoes Italy—the country of beauty, warmth, and the thrilling blend of chaos and grace. I'll return someday, whether to wander familiar streets or follow where life leads next.

Italy: The Slow Country That Moves Fast

People say Italy "moves slowly," but I beg to differ. Beneath the relaxed streets pulses fierce hustle. Take a Ferrari: it doesn't creep; it roars. Only those who've never felt that engine's fury would call Italy slow.

In Italy, life is a dance of contrasts—leisurely lunches followed by rapid sprints through cobblestone alleys. It's a place where time bends, where moments stretch and contract in a symphony of pace.

Slow? Well, only to those who haven't experienced its vibrant rhythm.

Returning Home: A Time Loop of Decline

Coming back felt like stepping into a broken time loop. Nothing improved—if anything, things worsened.

My Facebook feed became a theater of chaos—a place where incompetence doesn't just walk in through the door; it sets up a permanent residence. When people can't even clone fake accounts properly, how do we expect them to manage real technology? It's akin to handing someone a smartphone and hoping they don't use it to call themselves from the past. Digital tools meant to empower are now engines of confusion, operated by drivers who skipped the tutorial.

Regulations? Calling them that feels insulting—like calling a rusty bicycle a Ferrari. These "regulations" are ignored, exploited, or designed to benefit a select few. They're form without function, structure without backbone.

The Data-Driven Decline

Four weeks in, and the decline is steady—and accelerating. Logistics, tightly woven into many industries, exemplifies this fall. My time series analysis reveals the degradation is systemic, deterring unicorn companies eager for efficiency and scale.

Meanwhile, Chinese and Japanese firms navigate the chaos with adaptability, embracing local risks and managing complex demands. Their success shows it takes more than technical savvy; it requires rethinking business structures entirely.

Local Skills vs. Global Demands

The country's vast potential clashes with a widespread skills mismatch. Local talents often fall short of global standards. Take Timika's gold mining—the country's treasure—still poorly managed at the local level despite ample resources.

In an era of global connectivity and knowledge access, such incompetence is staggering. My data paints a grim odds of change because these problems are deep-rooted—infrastructure and human capital alike.

A Case Study in Logistical Chaos

Local delivery is emblematic of the mess:

  • Example 1: A delivery worker repeatedly demanded upfront payment on a COD order, breaking basic protocols.
  • Example 2: Following a worker's very instructions, my package still never arrived.
  • Example 3: Another worker doubted my order, suggesting my appearance made me an unlikely customer—blatantly unprofessional.
  • Example 4: A seller rejected a refund, claiming a quality-control check failed. The package was repackaged exactly as received, and the return procedure followed precisely. The real issue is clear: the product never met basic shipping standards before it left the warehouse.
  • Example 5: A seller insisted on processing the order despite being overwhelmed by workload—classic “we’re too busy to help you” logic.

These failures speak volumes: orders received, jobs bungled, customers disrespected. Companies hate complaints, demand proof, but won't fix their workers. If they can't handle risks, they should stick to offline stores. Denying this reality will ensure their stagnation.

When Expertise Becomes a Guess: The Medical Paradox

And just to prove that this place never runs out of surprises, consider the healthcare system—where the same disease receives wildly different diagnoses depending on which doctor you visit. I watched one patient navigate through different hospitals and specialists, all equally qualified, all utterly confident in their contradictory conclusions.

One doctor diagnosed kidney stones with absolute certainty. Other doctors examined the same patient, reviewed the same scans, and insisted with equal confidence that there were no kidney stones at all. Flip a coin, apparently, and you might get the right answer—or you might not. This isn't medicine; it's professional roulette, where your health depends on which expert you happen to consult first.

It's a perfect metaphor for systemic dysfunction: even when people are technically qualified, when accountability is absent and standards are negotiable, expertise becomes meaningless. The framework exists, but the foundation has crumbled.

The Circus of Leadership

As for the officials supposedly running this place—sometimes I wonder if they're doing any work at all. Or perhaps they've perfected the art of looking busy while accomplishing nothing, a skill that would earn them a standing ovation in any corporate playbook. They're feeding their families without feeding the country's future—a zero-sum game where everybody loses except them.

We love to say that a nation is poor when the gap between rich and poor grows too wide. Well, here, that gap is a canyon. And we’re not building bridges; we’re throwing rocks across it.

Even the places meant to lead by example—the top universities, for instance—are no better. I’ve worked at one of the “top ten” private institutions here. Let’s just say: it’s a circus that keeps running because chaos is the main attraction.

Our leaders love shiny new gadgets, imported tech, and expensive toys. But when they break? No one knows how to fix them. They’ve mastered the art of buying progress without understanding it.

The Unfixable Machine

Writing this, I hear the invisible strategists who want to silence voices like mine. But truth is louder.

Getting fired or replaced here is rarer than a unicorn on a skateboard—not because people perform well, but because accountability has gone on permanent vacation. You're either stuck in dusty inertia (where your job description hasn't changed since 1995) or the glitchy gig economy (where your paycheck has). Change is the national boogeyman, and responsibility? We've buried that so deep, archaeologists would struggle to find it.

If my rant backfires? So be it. It was an expensive mistake—and worth every penny.

Telecom and E-Commerce: A Staggering Paradox

Italy offers 5G with 300GB for €7.99—a far cry from Taiwan's €27.86 for less than 70GB or Indonesia's €7 for just 90GB of 4G. The disparity is so laughably absurd that it feels like someone's playing an elaborate prank on developing nations. For the same price, you could get a data plan in one country or a decent meal in another. The question isn't about value anymore—it's about basic fairness.

Italy's e-commerce, like Amazon in Pisa, impresses with speed and reliability. Jeff Bezos built an empire on such service. Meanwhile, back home, the popular COD option vanished from my and my mother's accounts—punishing customers instead of improving broken systems. This shortsightedness explains why decline persisted from 2018 to 2025.

Lost Youth in a Broken System

Here, youth aimlessly roam streets, revving broken vehicles—a symphony of wasted potential, each noise a cry for direction that nobody's bothering to hear. It's a show without meaning, performed for an audience of equally lost peers. This is what neglecting education breeds: a generation that knows the price of everything but the value of nothing, searching desperately for validation in all the wrong places because nobody bothered to teach them where the right ones are.

The Helper's Blind Spot

Humans are wired to help—it's in our DNA, essential for survival when we were huddled around campfires. Yet in complex modern life, this wiring misfires spectacularly. I call this the Helper's Blind Spot: that dangerous zone where good intentions meet incompetence, producing solutions that feel brilliant right up until they explode.

From Asia to Europe, I've witnessed well-meaning interventions transform solvable problems into unsalvageable disasters. A piece of misguided advice, delivered with genuine enthusiasm, can derail entire systems. Machines break predictably. Humans? They break in creative and imaginative ways. It's a bitter truth worth facing: good hearts don't always make good decisions.

The Trap of Imitation

Developing countries mimic developed ones with the enthusiasm of a freshman adopting the style of a senior—without understanding that the recipe for success doesn't translate across borders. Thriving nations have already paid the price of experimentation; developing ones rush to copy the answer key while skipping the actual learning process.

A shop across from my mother's clings to outdated practices like a sinking ship gripping driftwood—all because someone from a "developed" country suggested it would look "advanced." Blind trust in foreign blueprints is like wearing someone else's prescription glasses: even if they're perfect for them, you'll just walk into walls.

The Mirage of Advanced Technology

Online shops promise progress but often feel like chasing mirages in the desert—the closer you get, the faster it disappears. Infrastructure projects promised like saviors rarely materialize; environmental policies crafted in Geneva look nothing like the ground reality here; remote work culture sounds wonderful until you realize half your country doesn't have reliable electricity. The gap between imported ideals and local reality isn't a crack—it's a chasm carved by decades of neglect.

Learning and Building Our Own Path

Studying abroad isn't about taking selfies in front of famous landmarks—it's about understanding how wonders are actually built, brick by brick, system by system. China figured this out: send your people everywhere, absorb everything, then build it yourself. They didn't just copy; they reverse-engineered, adapted, and created their own versions.

Contrast this with gold mining in Timika. Decades later, we're still calling British experts to solve problems we should have mastered a generation ago. It's not a resource problem—we're sitting on literal mountains of wealth. It's a mindset problem. We've outsourced our confidence along with our expertise.

The Path Forward: Adaptive Innovation

Blind adoption is folly of the highest order—like buying a Ferrari when you live on roads that haven't been paved since the 1970s. Instead, we must embrace disciplined trial and error: smart experimentation that learns from every failure without repeating them.

Success lies not in following blueprints, but in sculpting solutions that fit our unique landscape. Our leaders, innovators, and communities know what we actually need—not what Silicon Valley thinks we should want. The process is simple: test, learn, iterate, and refine until the solution doesn't just work; until it sings in perfect harmony with our reality.

Closing Reflection

Developing nations stand at a crossroads, tempted by dazzling foreign blueprints but needing a different path. By daring to experiment wisely and build from within, we move from survival to true thriving—a legacy of innovation that we own.
