The Invisible Hand:
Phone Bot Farming and the New Shape of Digital Deception

When fake reviews, phantom customers, and automated engagement become standard business practice — who's really being fooled?

Personal Essay · Technology & Society · ~8 min read

There is a type of business that thrives in the background of your screen — invisible, profitable, and surprisingly ordinary. It does not make a product, offer a service, or solve a meaningful problem. It simply runs phones. Dozens, sometimes hundreds of them, stacked in racks or scattered across tables, each one endlessly scrolling, tapping, watching ads, and generating the illusion of human presence. This is phone bot farming, and it is far more widespread than most people realise.

The mechanics are deceptively simple. Phone bot farms are operations where multiple automated devices generate fake engagement on social media platforms — likes, follows, view counts, and comment activity that makes an account appear more popular than it actually is. The software uses scripts to mimic real user behaviour, helping to avoid detection by platform algorithms. What began as a grey-area side hustle has matured into a structured commercial operation. You can now purchase turnkey smartphone farming setups directly — suppliers on platforms like Accio openly sell bot farm hardware, treating it as an ordinary product category alongside phone accessories. The infrastructure has been fully commodified.

Particularly common across parts of Asia — though by no means confined there — the practice spans everything from passive income operations running reward apps around the clock to large-scale influence manipulation. In 2024, Ukraine's Security Service uncovered multiple Russian bot farms operating across several cities, allegedly running to generate political disinformation. The same technical infrastructure that inflates a product's star rating can, scaled up and pointed differently, be used to destabilise public information. The platforms being gamed often know this is happening. Sometimes, it seems, they are complicit.

What You're Actually Seeing

The Storefront That Isn't

Think about the last time you shopped on an e-commerce platform. You checked the product photos, scrolled through reviews, glanced at the star rating, maybe watched a short seller video. Each of those signals felt like evidence — proof that real people had bought this, tested it, vouched for it. But what if none of that was real? Or only less than half real? What if the reviews were written by people who had never seen the product, the star rating was generated by a bot farm, and the seller video was a staged performance by an actor hired to look like they were using the item? This is not a hypothetical scenario. It is a common reality in many online marketplaces.

The contrast between major regional platforms is instructive. Some marketplaces are noticeably noisier: products you have never clicked on push unsolicited video ads the moment the app opens; comment sections feel rehearsed; engagement numbers seem unnervingly round. Others feel more measured — products surface because they are relevant, and the experience has less of that frantic, overstimulated energy. The difference is not always a question of size or resources. It is often a question of what the platform has decided its metrics should look like, and how far it is willing to go to manufacture them.

"Imagine shopping on an e-commerce site: you check the description, star ratings, reviews, and seller details. But if those details are fake — what exactly did you just buy?"

The uncomfortable answer is that some sellers, despite their fabricated storefronts, still deliver perfectly good products. Others do not. The deception and the quality of the actual item are separate issues, which makes the whole thing harder to condemn cleanly. You were lied to. You might also have received exactly what you needed.

The System and Those Who Learn It

Gaming the Metrics

Phone bot farming is one expression of a much broader phenomenon: the recognition that metrics are not reality — they are representations of reality, and representations can be engineered. This is not a new insight. It is the fundamental principle behind all forms of marketing, public relations, and reputation management. But the scale and automation of phone bot farming take it to a new level — one where the line between genuine popularity and manufactured popularity becomes increasingly blurred.

This logic appears in unexpected places. The remarkable rise of several Chinese universities in global academic rankings is one instructive parallel. According to the Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2026, China now has five universities in the top 40 — up from three the previous year. It is a trajectory that invites genuine debate.

Sceptics see strategic metric optimization at work. Supporters point to real investment and measurable research output. THE's own analysis offers a more nuanced picture: Chinese institutions achieve among the highest research quality scores globally, yet they rank second from the bottom in the share of papers with international co-authors. As one researcher described it, China is doing exceptionally strong work at the frontiers of science — while a very long tail of lower-quality domestic research pulls the broader picture in the other direction. The headline number flatters a more complicated reality.

This is not unique to China — it is how metrics work everywhere. A number captures a slice of something real, optimized within a particular frame. The question of whether that constitutes genuine excellence or sophisticated presentation depends heavily on which slice you choose to look at. What is clear is that these institutions have studied the system — its inputs, weightings, and blind spots — and acted on what they found. Whether that deserves admiration, scrutiny, or both is a question worth sitting with rather than settling too quickly.

When It Becomes a Weapon

From Bot Farms to Troll Farms: The State-Sponsored Dimension

The same infrastructure that inflates a product's star rating can, pointed in a different direction and scaled up with state resources, be used to destabilize a country. This is not a hypothetical. It has already happened — repeatedly, and with documented evidence.

In August 2022, Ukraine's Security Service (SBU) dismantled a bot farm operating over one million fake online accounts, alongside social media networks with an audience exceeding 400,000 users. The operation had been running disinformation campaigns targeting Ukraine's political and military leadership — fabricating narratives about internal conflict between senior officials, and content aimed at discrediting public figures. The SBU identified the network's leader as a Russian citizen living in Kyiv, operating on the instructions of a Ukrainian political party. The alleged cybercriminals were backed by Russia, the SBU said. This was not a side hustle. It was an information operation, running on the exact same botnet logic as a fake product review campaign — just pointed at a government instead of a marketplace.

It is also worth noting that this was not Ukraine's first encounter with domestic political bot abuse. In 2020, Facebook removed dozens of fake accounts linked to a Ukrainian marketing group for spreading offensive content during elections. Later that year, it removed a further network of accounts with alleged links to an established political party. Bots tied to political PR have become, in Ukraine, almost a standard feature of the electoral landscape.

"The same technical infrastructure that inflates a product's star rating can, scaled up and pointed differently, destabilise a government. The hardware is identical. Only the target changes."

The United States has faced its own version of this. In July 2024, the Department of Justice announced the disruption of a Russian AI-powered social media operation, working with partners in Canada and the Netherlands to seize domains used to register fake email addresses. Those addresses were used to create 968 automated accounts on X — a bot farm, in the technical sense. The accounts were traced back to an operation originally organized by an editor at Russia Today (RT) and later taken over by a private intelligence organization linked to Russia's FSB. What made this case particularly significant was the software: RT affiliates had deployed an AI tool called Meliorator to generate convincing fake American personas at scale, complete with profile photos, biographical details, and fabricated location data. The bots were designed in three tiers — high-activity profiles for spreading content, minimal accounts for generating likes, and a third tier built from stolen real-person data to look maximally authentic. As FBI Director Christopher Wray stated at the time, Russia intended to use the farm to spread AI-generated disinformation and "undermine our partners in Ukraine," scaling their influence operations through artificial intelligence.

The Troll Farm: Bot Farming's Human Cousin

Bot farms automate the creation of fake online presences. Troll farms engage in similar yet different activities. They employ real people to post, comment, argue, and manipulate. These employees often work in shifts in purpose-built office facilities and are given daily output quotas. For more information, see the preprint published on Arxiv by researchers from the Universidad Carlos III de Madrid in Spain, titled "Troll Farms". The most documented example is Russia's Internet Research Agency (IRA), which was exposed through the Mueller investigation and subsequent US Senate Intelligence Committee reports. The St. Petersburg–based operation employed hundreds of people to create fake American social media identities, participate in political debates, organize real-world events, and disseminate divisive content across Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, and Reddit ahead of the 2016 US election (See Russian trolls who interfered in 2016 U.S. election also made ad money, report says).

More detailed information about the Brexit referendum and the 2016 U.S. Presidential Election is available in a 2021 European Economic Review paper titled "Social media, sentiment and public opinions: Evidence from# Brexit and# USElection". It examines how bots and humans interacted on Twitter, the sentiment of tweets, and the potential impact of bots on vote outcomes. The American Political Science Review in 2017 published a paper titled "How the Chinese Government Fabricates Social Media Posts for Strategic Distraction, Not Engaged Argument". This review thoroughly examines how the Chinese government uses human-operated troll accounts to flood social media with content that distracts from sensitive issues instead of engaging in genuine discourse. Imagine this: The Chinese government is suspected of hiring up to two million people, including Communist Party members, journalists, academics, and social media users, to secretly insert large quantities of pseudonymous writings — formerly known as "internet commentary" or an "army of online commentators" — into streams of real social media posts. This makes it appear as if the writings are the genuine opinions of ordinary people, such as famous bloggers, lawyers, comedians, marketing executives, or professors.

These cases all have one thing in common: the use of human labor to create a more convincing and adaptable form of inauthentic presence. This presence can respond to real-time events, craft nuanced narratives, and interact with genuine users in ways that bots cannot. Though these operations are more expensive and logistically complex than bot farms, they can be more effective at influencing public opinion and sowing discord due to their human touch. While bot farms rely on volume and automation, troll farms rely on quality and sustained human performance. A bot account can like a post, but a troll account can write a convincing, emotionally resonant comment that gets shared ten thousand times. The two approaches are often combined. Bot networks amplify content seeded by human trolls. Together, these methods constitute what researchers now call "coordinated inauthentic behavior": the systematic creation of the appearance of organic public opinion.

Bot Farm vs. Troll Farm

Bot farm: Automated accounts performing repetitive actions at scale — likes, views, follows, reviews. Low cost, high volume, detectable by pattern analysis.

Troll farm: Real human operators maintaining fake personas, crafting targeted content designed to pass as organic opinion. Higher cost, harder to detect, greater influence per account.

Combined operations: Bots amplify what trolls seed. The result is artificial consensus — the manufactured appearance that a viewpoint, product, or narrative has widespread genuine support.

At first glance, e-commerce fake reviews, the Brexit referendum, the 2016 U.S. presidential election, Ukrainian political disinformation, Chinese fake online debates, and Russian election interference may seem unrelated. However, they are connected by a shared logic. Metrics measure perceived reality. Perceived reality influences decisions. These decisions can be manipulated by engineering the metrics. The only variables are the scale of ambition and the available resources to pursue it.

What You See on Your Feed

The Cloned Influencer: Content Farms in Your Scroll

If you pay attention while scrolling through Facebook Reels, you'll notice the same video appearing under different usernames. The face is the same. The script is the same. The caption has been slightly altered. Nothing about it feels original because it isn't. What you're watching is a content farm in action. One video is duplicated across dozens, or even hundreds, of fake accounts, each of which monetizes the same views as if they were unique creators. Scary, isn't it? Yes.

This is one of the more visible aspects of the bot farm industry. The economics are straightforward. Platforms like Facebook pay content creators based on how much time viewers spend watching their content and how much they engage with it. For instance, if you clone a moderately viral video across fifty accounts, you generate fifty revenue streams from a single piece of content. The "influencer" whose face you keep seeing may not know that their likeness is being used. In some cases, the face itself is AI-generated and designed to look convincing. Isn't that scary?

If you're on the lookout, the telltale signs are usually consistent: oddly stilted delivery and off-kilter topics. For instance, there's the "cooking expert" who doesn't seem to know how to cook and the "financial advisor" whose advice is so vague it's useless. Spamouflage accounts reveal themselves through stilted English and strange word choices, showing that the persona is manufactured rather than genuine. The same pattern shows up in commercial content farms. E-commerce platforms are a simple example of this. One large group is divided into multiple sellers with no detailed information about their offline store or location — just a profile picture, a name, and a product listing. The same video is posted across multiple accounts, each with a different username and slightly altered captions. The content is identical, but the engagement is multiplied across accounts. If you've seen it once and then again, you're not imagining it — you're watching the same content farm harvest the same video twice.

"The same face. The same script. A different account name. If you've seen it once and then again, you're not imagining it — you're watching the same content farm harvest the same video twice."

Spamouflage: When Content Farming Goes Political

The logical endpoint of content farming — when the goal shifts from ad revenue to political influence — is something researchers have been documenting for years. The most thoroughly documented example is Spamouflage, a Chinese state-linked disinformation network that NBC Montana reported on in September 2024 as part of wider coverage of the US election cycle.

Spamouflage earned its name through its habit of spreading large amounts of seemingly unrelated content alongside disinformation — camouflaging political messaging inside otherwise ordinary-seeming social media activity. Graphika, the online tracking firm that traced the network, described it as one of the world's largest covert influence operations, run by Chinese state actors, that had become increasingly aggressive in attempting to infiltrate US political conversations ahead of the 2024 election.

The mechanics are recognisable from the content farm playbook, just pointed at a different target. Spamouflage accounts didn't post much original content — instead, they used platforms like X and TikTok to recycle and repost content from far-right and far-left users. One account, operating under the name "Harlan," presented itself as a New York Army veteran supporting Trump, then later shifted to a 31-year-old Republican in Florida — his profile picture was likely created using AI. The operation ran accounts targeting both Democrats and Republicans simultaneously, with the goal being not to support any particular candidate, but to deepen existing divisions. It's a reminder that online influence operations are often a numbers game: the more accounts, the more content, the better the chance that one specific post goes viral.

In August 2023, Meta announced a takedown of nearly 9,000 accounts and pages associated with Spamouflage, with activity traced across YouTube, TikTok, and other platforms. The network had also allegedly created a fake research paper attributing COVID-19 to the United States and used deepfake video technology to fabricate statements by Chinese dissidents — including a fabricated video of a real person being made to say things they never said. By the 2024 Taiwan presidential election, Spamouflage was using generative AI to create memes and videos featuring AI television anchors to attack a presidential candidate with fabricated corruption allegations.

The Content Farm Spectrum

At one end: cloned Facebook Reels monetising ad revenue under fake influencer accounts. At the other: state-sponsored deepfake videos targeting election candidates. The distance between them is not as large as it appears. Both depend on the same core insight — that platforms reward perceived engagement, not genuine engagement, and that perceived engagement can be manufactured at scale.

When Fraud Becomes Ambient

The New Shape of Harm

Modern digital crime does not usually resemble traditional crime. There are no broken windows or stolen wallets, nor are there obvious victims who know they have been harmed. Instead, the harm is diffuse and slow, spread across thousands of slightly misleading data points and slightly less trustworthy platforms. The definition of harm has shifted from direct, identifiable damage to more abstract concepts like the erosion of trust and authenticity. No lives are taken. However, something valuable is slowly being destroyed: trust in reviews and ratings and the assumption that what you see online reflects reality.

Is artificially inflating customer counts or generating fake reviews considered theft? Legally speaking, the answer is unclear in most jurisdictions. However, ethically speaking, the answer is closer to yes. The transaction is based on false information. Manufactured evidence shapes the buyer's decision to purchase a product and which product to choose, as well as the buyer's decision to trust the seller. This is manipulation, even if the product itself is fine.

Social media amplifies this effect. Rather than showing us the world, these platforms show us a curated and often fabricated version of it—one optimized for engagement rather than truth. Although "engagement" is an uninspiring term, it's useful and correlates highly with almost all organizational metrics. Phone bot farming is a blunt, mercenary extension of the same principle. While social platforms manipulate perception to sell advertising, bot farmers manipulate perception to sell products, inflate rankings, and accumulate reward credits. The underlying logic is identical.

"No lives are taken. But something valuable is slowly destroyed: trust — in reviews, in ratings, in the assumption that what you see online reflects something real."

Regulation and What Comes Next

The Law Is Always Late

Regulators are not oblivious to this issue. Consumer protection agencies in various countries (some of which are nonfunctional) have started to pursue cases involving fake reviews and manipulated metrics. When pressed, platform companies announce new detection systems. Occasionally, someone gets fined. However, the pace of enforcement consistently lags behind the rate at which this practice occurs.

This is more than just an administrative failure. Rather, it reflects a structural problem. The systems being exploited were designed based on outdated assumptions about human behavior. For instance, a review platform built on the premise that reviews come from actual customers has limited defenses against an operator running three hundred phones from a rented room. The rules were written for a different world.

Eventually, legislation will catch up. It always does. In the meantime, however, the gap between what is legal and what is ethical continues to widen. Those most affected by this discrepancy are ordinary consumers trying to make everyday decisions based on information they reasonably believed to be trustworthy. The issue is not only how to regulate this practice, but also how to recalibrate our expectations of online information in the meantime.

A Personal Note

What It Means to Know This

There is something clarifying about understanding how these systems work — even if what it clarifies is mostly unsettling. Once you know that review counts can be automated, that star ratings can be purchased, that video view numbers can be generated by a room full of idle phones, it becomes harder to take any of these signals at face value. The instinct to trust flickers, and then recalibrates.

This is not entirely bad. It's probably overdue to be more skeptical of metrics, including academic rankings. Numbers have always only partially represented something more complex, yet we have been too willing to treat them as neutral facts. If bot farming has taught us anything, it's that a high score and genuine quality are not the same. Conflating them is a choice, not a truth. As difficult as it may be to accept, a five-star rating does not mean that a product is good. It simply means it has a five-star rating. The difference is important.

Admiring the ingenuity of the people who built these systems does not mean endorsing them. The technical skill involved, such as understanding platform architectures, designing automation pipelines, and evading detection, is real and significant. However, technical skill deployed in service of mass deception is not admirable simply because it is clever. Cleverness is morally neutral. What you do with it is not. Know this, and you will see the world differently. You will notice the invisible hand of bot farms everywhere—in your feed, your shopping, and your news. The question is whether you will also recognize the human choices behind them and understand their implications for the future of online trust. Welcome to the new normal of digital deception. This new normal is more dangerous because it involves many professionals, but it is also more insidious because it is invisible. Bots are not the problem. They are just tools. The problem lies in the system that rewards them and in the people who choose to use them.

The world is moving fast. The gap between innovation and accountability widens every few months. Some of what currently sits in that gap will eventually be regulated, prosecuted, or simply made unprofitable by better detection. Some of it will become so normalised that we stop noticing it. The more urgent question — the one that individuals, platforms, and policymakers all face — is which outcome we are actually working toward.
