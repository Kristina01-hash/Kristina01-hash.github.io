Personal Observations

Real experiences from Indonesian urban and semi-rural settings

Several years ago, after multiple incidents, CCTV was installed throughout a home to deter disturbances and protect property. The expectation was that visible cameras would change behavior—but they did not. Loud noise late at night, petty mischief, and theft continued unabated.

The small home-based business was even required to close at a certain hour, despite being in a residential-commercial area. The business complied, but new problems emerged: some individuals treated the street like their personal playground—loitering, making noise, and vandalizing property, confident that no one would intervene.

Wrongdoing persisted because enforcement was inconsistent. The cameras recorded, but accountability was absent.

Case Study: Over three months, individuals who frequently passed a ruko (commercial shop row) appeared aware of camera locations. Footage would be missing, incomplete, or unavailable precisely when they passed—not random technical failure, but knowledge of blind spots and confidence that no one would act.
This pattern suggests not just technical vulnerability, but systemic follow-through failures that embolden misconduct.

Impact on Livelihoods

The problem extends beyond urban residential areas. Small-scale theft and vandalism threaten farmers, shopkeepers, and service providers across Indonesia.

Rural Impact: A farmer reported repeated theft of essential farming tools when he briefly left his land unattended—tools critical to his livelihood.

Urban Impact: A small barber shop lost essential equipment overnight—clippers and supplies representing years of investment.

In both cases, the issue was not poverty or opportunity alone—it was confidence that misconduct would go unpunished. When consequences vanish, responsibility dies. Citizens, (small) business owners, and working families are left exposed while some individuals operate as if untouchable, shielded by systemic indifference. This isn't random crime—it's enabled disorder, born from the certainty that authorities either won't respond or lack the capacity to act. Until that confidence is shattered through consistent enforcement, the cycle perpetuates.

Root Cause: Weak Accountability

At the heart of these issues is systemic failure. Laws exist, but they often protect certain individuals while ordinary citizens suffer. Imported technology—CCTV, smart systems, or digital reporting platforms—fails to drive progress because enforcement is inconsistent and selective.

When law serves a few rather than the many, social discipline collapses. Disorder becomes normalized. Cities stagnate—not from lack of resources, but from the erosion of fairness and responsibility.
  • Under-resourced monitoring and rapid-response teams
  • Insufficient technical capacity to maintain systems or analyze evidence
  • Bureaucratic hurdles that delay or prevent follow-up actions
  • Lack of transparent reporting on enforcement outcomes
  • Cameras record, complaints are filed, but follow-up is sporadic. Worstly, offline or unreported incidents are ignored entirely. Reporting something not only like throwing a stone into the void, but you also need to pay some money to make it disappear (sarcasm 😉)
  • In some cases, irresponsible authorities or individuals have chosen to replace themselves entirely with incompetent personnel to avoid accountability.
  • Corruption that undermines the entire system—officers collaborating with criminal elements for personal gain, turning enforcement into a facade.

A Warning to Authorities

Critical Warning: Taxpayers fund public salaries to enforce the law, not overlook misconduct or tolerate disorder. Let me be clear: taxpayers pay you to ensure their safety. They don't pay you to ignore the situation or look the other way. In case you forgot, they're the ones paying your salary. Wake up, and do your job.

When officials fail to act, they are complicit through inaction. Negligence is not harmless—it becomes a form of exploitation. Scrutiny will shift to those entrusted with protection but who refuse to act. Thus, public trust erodes, and disorder spreads unchecked.

Cameras, reporting systems, and technology only matter when institutions follow through. Otherwise, they merely highlight failures that everyone can see. Having knowledge of distinguishing valid and invalid reports is crucial. If authorities cannot discern genuine reports from false ones, they risk wasting resources and eroding public trust. This inability to differentiate can lead to skepticism among citizens, who may feel that their concerns are not taken seriously.

Limitations of Technology

Even the most advanced CCTV system is ineffective without skilled personnel. Technology without capable personnel is symbolic—cameras sit idle, reports go unanswered, and incidents accumulate while public trust erodes.

❌ Current Reality (Indonesia)

Monitoring centers are chronically understaffed and unable to effectively cover large urban areas. A quick Google search will provide more information, though I don't know the exact statistic. Officers often lack specialized training in CCTV analysis and digital evidence handling. In fact, despite being affordable, CCTV is not widely used. Reports languish for months or years—or are quietly closed as if they never existed. Follow-up is inconsistent at best. As a result, public trust deteriorates, and cameras become mere decorations rather than tools for protection. Here, accountability is the exception, not the norm. Simply put, they are wasted investments. Even worse, they are just for show, pretending that something is being done. It's a tragic waste of resources. The same may be true for other public security officers.

✓ Working Models (Taiwan)

Police receive systematic technical training and work in adequately staffed control rooms. Response times are measured and publicly reported. Lost items—even expensive electronics—are frequently returned through a reliable lost-and-found system. Accountability is normalized, not exceptional. I ever biked through Zhongli early morning and passing by a police station, I saw officers actively monitoring CCTV feeds and coordinating responses. This proactive approach fosters community trust and deters misconduct. Try to report something there, it will be handled properly like in a minute, everything is so fast and reliable. You don't even notice some incidents because they are resolved so quickly.

Key difference: It's not about budget or technology—it's about institutional culture. When accountability is embedded in daily operations, even modest resources produce reliable outcomes.

Common systemic limitations include:

  • Insufficient staffing to monitor large urban areas effectively
  • Limited technical expertise to maintain systems or interpret surveillance data
  • Bureaucratic procedures that delay or obstruct enforcement actions
  • Lack of transparency in reporting enforcement outcomes to the public
  • Inconsistent follow-up on reported incidents, leaving victims without resolution

  • Technological upgrades without parallel investments in human capacity and training

When Incentives Are Not Enough

Even generous fiscal measures cannot compensate for weak accountability and systemic limitations.

Example: Based on my extensive personal experience, Indonesia proudly offers tax deductions in the lofty range of 20–35%—which, quite impressively, almost rivals the 10–37% range of a highly advanced, developed nation like the United States (google it yourself). This generosity also comfortably outshines Taiwan’s modest ~7.5% and Italy’s roughly 13%. And yet, somehow, all this fiscal generosity hasn’t translated into meaningful progress—perhaps due to minor details like a shortage of personnel, gaps in technical expertise, and enforcement that occasionally remembers to show up. Naturally, this leads to the inevitable conclusion: the Taiwanese tax system is the best. Based on personal experience (again), Taiwan’s system is clearly the best—efficient, functional, and blissfully lacking the need for excuses. Regarding Taiwan's tax system, based on my personal experience (including an accident that became a valuable lesson), I can confidently say it's among the most efficient I've encountered—remarkably fast and reliable.

Citizens witness the consequences: CCTV systems installed but ignored, small businesses left unprotected, complaints unaddressed. Fiscal incentives without accountability create only cosmetic compliance—where appearances matter more than outcomes (the real failure).

For real progress, incentives must be paired with:

  • Comprehensive training programs: Regular, updated training for personnel on surveillance technology, evidence handling, and community engagement
  • Robust accountability frameworks: Implement not only performance metrics but also consequences for negligence or misconduct
  • Transparent reporting mechanisms: Conduct regular audits with publicly accessible reports on system effectiveness, response times, and case resolution rates
  • Ethical recruitment standards: Prioritize hiring personnel genuinely committed to public service and ethical enforcement—not just filling quotas
  • Modern technological integration: Embrace AI-driven analytics, community reporting platforms, and smart infrastructure. Clinging to outdated systems in an era of technological advancement is counterproductive—you cannot build a smart city on obsolete foundations, just as you cannot construct durable roads using antiquated methods. Progress demands modern knowledge and tools.
  • Most importantly, a cultural shift: Foster a culture of integrity, responsibility, and public service within enforcement agencies. Technology is merely a tool; the true change comes from people committed to making a difference.
  • You can train people to be more intelligent, competent, and accountable. But you cannot train stupidity, incompetence, and irresponsibility. Manipulators will always find a way to exploit weaknesses. Thus, investing in technology without addressing human and institutional factors is a recipe for wasted resources and continued disorder.

Reality Check: No city becomes "smart" by slapping cameras on poles while running bug-ridden systems from two decades ago.

It's 2026. The world has moved on. Clinging to broken, outdated infrastructure isn't frugality—it's negligence. Every glitchy system, every untrained operator, every ignored report is a choice. A choice to maintain the status quo of dysfunction rather than pursue genuine progress.

Wake up. "Smart city" isn't a label you buy—it's a standard you earn through competence, accountability, and systems that actually work. Until then, you're just decorating dysfunction with buzzwords. Conclusion: Progress Requires Accountability

The issues form a clear chain: weak accountability, selective law enforcement, lack of human capacity, technological stagnation, and ineffective fiscal incentives. Cameras, infrastructure, and imported systems cannot fix governance failures alone. The solution lies in strengthening institutions, training personnel, and embedding accountability into daily operations.

Real progress begins not with technology, but with consequences. Until laws are applied equally, enforcement is predictable, and public workers are fully accountable, every "upgrade" will remain cosmetic—and the decline of public trust will continue. Pretending otherwise is not just foolish—it’s irresponsible (sarcasm intended).

Key Takeaway: Technology can make things “safer,” sure—but real progress comes from predictable enforcement, genuine accountability, and treating citizens as rights-holders, not walking ATMs. In short: control without accountability isn’t order—it’s tyranny in a very polite outfit. Someone has to say it, so the rest can get the wake-up call and a little much-needed training 😉

The Path Forward: Pair investments in monitoring technologies with adequate staffing, transparent procedures, public reporting, and meaningful community engagement. Citizens can innovate, install, and observe—but without consistent enforcement and trained personnel, the system remains broken. Thank me later if you implement this advice 😉

Final Thought: Transparency and accountability are not optional—they are the foundation for trust, safety, and progress. Who knows somebody is watching, but no one acts?
