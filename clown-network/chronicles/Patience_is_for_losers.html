I

The Street, the Spark, and the Subsidy I Never Signed Up For

It started with a small, disheartening observation: a few inconsiderate individuals on the street. Watching them, I thought to myself, "It is easy to see why decline manifests in places like this." That modest moment was enough to ignite something larger.

From there, my thoughts drifted inward. I have long believed that my dedication has added genuine value to this nation — through institutions built, enterprises launched, across both digital and physical domains. Yet, despite these efforts — often feeling like reluctant volunteer work 🤣 — I now see my financial and academic contributions as unintended subsidies for systemic inefficiency and a culture too often lacking in integrity. What began as enthusiastic support at personal cost has gradually become a burden. Dishonesty, it turns out, is not a bug in the system. It is the system.

This is not a complaint. It is an observation — the kind that sharpens itself into something more useful the longer you sit with it.

II

Three Countries, Three Bureaucracies, One Uncomfortable Mirror

Taiwan: When Systems Actually Work

Entering a Taiwanese government office is, improbably, a transformative experience. Procedures unfold swiftly and flawlessly. Each step follows a clear, logical structure that leaves no space for confusion. Staff carry out their duties with quiet confidence and professionalism — no theatre, no deflection, no one pretending the supervisor is unavailable.

The implicit message of every interaction is something like:

"We require you to report and update all necessary information during your stay. What matters most is that the data is accurate and valid. We recognise that your time is precious and appreciate the effort you take to visit us."

I never walked out of a Taiwanese office feeling either disappointed or encumbered. During my PhD there, not a single day was lost to bureaucratic limbo. Even in postdoctoral work, when external factors occasionally complicated matters, the core efficiency of their systems remained unwavering. Administrative and institutional networks are seamlessly interconnected — universities, workplaces, government offices, all in quiet coordination. Transparency ensures that processes are smooth, purposeful, and reliable.

In Taiwan, patience is almost redundant. Because things simply work.

Italy: Charming, Chaotic, and Entirely on Its Own Schedule

Italy retains an undeniable charm wrapped in languid, unhurried beauty. Perhaps it's only the country's bureaucracy that moves at a snail's pace — but who can say for certain? 🤣

A colleague once told me plainly: "If you know someone in Italian bureaucracy, you'll complete your task faster. If not, it will take time." Holy shit — and that's entirely true. The system runs on a "who you know" basis rather than any structured, equitable process.

No queue numbers. Little organisation. A prevailing sense of unpredictability baked into every step. I once spent nearly an entire day attempting to collect an identity card, only to reach the office doors as they were about to close. Entry was granted not by procedure but by the casual intervention of another visitor — a man whose nationality I couldn't place, Indian or Pakistani perhaps, who simply said to the officer: "Let her in."

In Taiwan, that officer would have looked at him and said: "Who the hell are you, trying to dictate what we do?" But Italy is not Taiwan. The idea of bypassing rules through casual suggestion is not an anomaly there — it is the culture. Holy fucking country. 🤣

The aura of that immigration office was negative, full of a particular exhausted hostility that seeps into you if you linger too long. Italy outshines Indonesia in many respects, and I say that as someone from a nation where regulations can feel equally inconsistent. But Italy at least has a whimsical predictability to its dysfunction — a rhythm you can eventually learn to navigate. It is amusing, in small doses. Even occasionally endearing.

Indonesia: Erratic, Performative, and Occasionally Absurd

Indonesian institutions often evoke a similar feeling to Italy's, but without the charm. Each official seems to be adhering to their own personal rulebook, and if you happen to know the right procedures — or, more precisely, the right people — your process may proceed smoothly. Without that familiarity, you will navigate the system at an agonisingly slow pace, where every step feels prolonged and unnecessarily cumbersome.

Take the banks. Indonesian banks project an image of seamless transactions, yet in practice almost invariably fail to meet those promises. Among them, only BCA demonstrates consistent delivery aligned with its marketing. The rest specialise in a particular form of institutional torture: redirecting you from one branch to another, each claiming that the next location can handle your request, until you eventually return to the very branch you started from. This circular journey is not incompetence in isolation — it is a system of incompetence, functioning as designed.

In government offices — including those in the capital — the disorganisation I encountered was remarkable, both exasperating and, in its own peculiar way, amusing. Staff seemed uncertain of their own responsibilities, frequently deferring to supervisors who were not even present. The environment felt uncoordinated, inefficient, and at times absurdly comic. This deeply embedded disorganisation stretches from Jakarta to smaller cities, where inefficiency and a lack of accountability have become cultural norms rather than embarrassments.

Were I to document this phenomenon fully, it could fill an entire series of volumes titled How Not to Complete a Task — a comprehensive guide to the art of deflecting responsibility and offering nothing but calls for "patience" to customers, clients, and colleagues alike.

One telling example: the incident at Alfamart Pane Tonga, where even cancelling a single item required approval from an absent superior — a so-called pejabat. This is not an exception. This is the model. People consciously hired but denied the authority to execute even the most basic functions. A vicious cycle of stagnation, in which patience is demanded not as a virtue but as a mask for persistent dysfunction.

And then there's the supposedly prestigious private bank with the glossy, "elegant" advertising. The reality? A young customer service representative, armed with nothing but arrogance and hollow explanations, rejected my straightforward credit card request with excuses that bordered on the absurd. A carefully staged façade masking a hollow core. Marketing projecting reliability; operations delivering chaos.

I often imagine bank executives — or any institutional leadership, really — observing their employees without warning, like gamblers testing their luck at an unpredictable table. 🤣 Such unannounced scrutiny would finally reveal the spontaneous, haphazard approach to work currently going unnoticed. My observation is that they'd realise, perhaps with some shock, that they've been hiring staff with poor quality and lacking integrity. And the main taxation office? Full of rubbish too. I wonder sometimes if that truly reflects the quality of people in this country — from unanswered simple questions to an entire institutional landscape of idiots. They never knew my curiosity was dangerous. 🤣

III

What Bureaucracy Actually Reveals

The contrast between Taiwan on one hand, and Italy and Indonesia on the other, illuminates a simple truth: bureaucracy is not just paperwork. It is a mirror of a nation's values.

Where honesty and efficiency prevail, patience becomes unnecessary — processes complete themselves, tasks are executed with precision, and respect for time is ingrained rather than performed. Where inefficiency and informality dominate, patience is not invited; it is demanded — a polite endurance test for systemic failures no one intends to fix.

Italy's inefficiency has an almost whimsical predictability. It moves to a rhythm that is entirely its own, as if time itself agreed to slow down within its walls. Indonesia's tends toward erratic chaos — unpredictable, uncoordinated, occasionally amusing, and ultimately exhausting. Both are tolerable in small doses. Over time, they erode one's appetite for progress.

This is worth observing on a global scale too. Chinese institutions have risen impressively in the world university rankings, surpassing some historically esteemed universities through deliberate strategy: substantial investment in education, sending large numbers of students abroad to acquire advanced knowledge, and building a growing capacity for innovation and original research. I remain cautiously observant of the long-term durability of these achievements — but there is elegance in their method. They learn from their missteps and improve without complaint. Criticise them and they do not waste time on argument; they simply continue working diligently and silently. 🤣 Such disciplined resilience is its own quiet strength.

The enduring dominance of the United States and Europe in producing world-changing innovations is hardly surprising, considering the centuries of legacy, institutional stability, and historical head start their universities enjoy. This privilege is reflected in the very rhythm of daily life — including in the consistency of their manufactured goods. American and European products command higher prices because they are designed as long-term investments, prioritising durability, reliability, and quality. There is something instructive, even beautiful, in a thing built to last.

Indonesia's global standing continues to lag significantly behind — a reality that still draws a wry smile on a quiet Saturday evening. 🤣

IV

Patience: A Word I Recognise But Will Never Embrace

P-A-T-I-E-N-C-E

Patience, to me, is not a celebrated virtue. It is a construct often weaponised to excuse inefficiency, delay, and incompetence. It is the quiet endurance of systems that crawl when they should stride — the polite mask for a reality I refuse to accept.

In environments where honesty and efficiency reign — like Taiwan — patience is rendered almost irrelevant. Processes flow, tasks are completed, and respect for time takes precedence over ceremonial waiting. I never once witnessed anyone there say one thing and deliver another. That straightforwardness feels both rare and invaluable.

The secret to the success of companies like NVIDIA and AMD is, I think, related to this. Their founders — with their Taiwanese heritage — have never concealed their early missteps or limitations. They embraced them openly, turning failure into fuel for growth. Patience isn't a virtue they extol; their focus is relentlessly practical: learn, adapt, improve, keep moving forward. Inefficiency is simply unacceptable.

Ask for patience in a system that doesn't deserve it, and you are not demonstrating virtue. You are covering for someone else's inability — or unwillingness — to do their job. Why not save those words for yourself? Simply work. Deliver results promptly. Do not demand tolerance for unnecessary delay.

It's not my fault God forgot to give me patience. My parents always encouraged me to be in a hurry and work non-stop. I spent years in a fast-paced, highly efficient environment among diligent and driven people. Expecting me to suddenly embrace patience feels almost absurd. 🤣 My instinct is always towards action — right or wrong is secondary, because in time, I will learn to make it right.

If Italy, Indonesia, or any individual wants to praise my impatience, I am more than happy to accept it. I'll proudly declare: "I'm impatient especially when surrounded by idiots."

If there is any appropriate test of my patience, it might be walking 25,000 steps in a day — at least that demands effort, persistence, and results. Better to test endurance on a tangible challenge than squander it on the incompetence of others. 🤣

V

Education, Honesty, and the Only Way Out

Education is important. Without learning, no true achievement is possible — you idiot. 🤣

I say this without irony. One can never be so well-educated as to fully grasp the depth and subtle deception behind the concept of patience. Knowledge is layered and structured, with certain forms holding greater power than others. This hierarchy determines who can innovate, lead, and create lasting foundations for progress. Knowledge surpasses religious observance in its capacity to transform lives — faith may offer comfort and guidance, but you cannot build a thriving society by relying solely on prayer or frequenting temples, mosques, and churches. True advancement demands the disciplined pursuit of learning and the application of that learning to the real world.

For those fortunate enough to be born wealthy: congratulations, your parents' money can shield you from the consequences of neglecting learning. You can hire intelligent professionals to execute ideas conceived by others, acquired with wealth you inherited. You might even purchase a prestigious degree from a top university, not through merit but through simple payment. Their intellectual shortcomings matter little — they can buy the competence they lack, and their children may continue in the same pattern, coasting on resources rather than skill.

For everyone else, dismissing the value of education is not a luxury they can afford. 🤣

My journals start as scattered fragments — but, darling, do you really think professional writers craft their books in perfect sequence? 🤣 The creative process is often gloriously messy. Even the most advanced systems begin in disarray before refinement brings coherence. Disorder is not a flaw. It is a natural stage of creation. Thanks to technology, lies are now easily found and detected. Only foolish or ignorant individuals would embarrass themselves at this level of intelligence.

Closing

Honesty as the Only Non-Negotiable

The most developed nations are distinguished by the straightforward honesty of their people. That integrity underpins their systems, their interactions, and the trust that allows progress to flourish. Where honesty prevails, efficiency follows. Where it doesn't, patience is demanded — an imposed endurance test, a polite mask for delays that should never have existed in the first place.

From that modest street-side moment to these reflections on global education and the trajectory of nations, the pattern is clear: personal behaviour, organisational integrity, and the durability of long-term structures are deeply intertwined. They shape whether a nation rises, falters, or simply endures quietly.

As for me — patience is a chain. I prefer to break it.

Honesty, however, is non-negotiable. It lightens the weight we carry, resolves disputes, and spares us the exhausting pretence of defending the indefensible. In a world where time is finite and honesty cannot be compromised, patience is a word I recognise — but will never, not once, embrace.

To the universe: if there's ever a room hosting a patience test, I'm out. I'll be in the jungle, where the only tests are those of nature itself. Or perhaps I'll just be sleeping. 🤣
